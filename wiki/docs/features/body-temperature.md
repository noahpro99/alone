Your body runs at a temperature you have to manage — too cold and you freeze, too hot and you cook — and it quietly drives your thirst, hunger, and stamina alongside its own dangers.

## Reading the temperature square

To the right of the corner meters is a **temperature square** with a small **trend arrow**:

- **Blue** — you're cold; **darker blue** means dangerously cold.
- **Green** — comfortable, right where you want to be.
- **Red** — you're hot; **darker red** means dangerously hot.
- The **arrow** shows the direction you're heading: warming up or cooling down. It shows the trend before the color itself changes.

## What drives your temperature

Your body temperature is pushed around by your surroundings:

- **Biome** — snowy taigas and tundras chill you; deserts and badlands bake you.
- **Weather** — rain and storms chill you fast.
- **Season** — winter shifts everything colder, summer warmer.
- **Time of day** — the open night sky is cold.
- **Wetness** — being wet or submerged makes cold far worse (see below).

## The danger bands

### Cold → hypothermia

Linger in the cold and you first **slow down**, then start taking **freezing damage**. Cold also has knock-on costs before it's outright deadly: below a hard chill your **stamina recovers at half rate** (you're stiff and shivering) and your body **burns extra food** to hold its heat.

**Cold *and* wet gives you a chill.** The real danger isn't dry cold — it's the **wet-cold combination** (soaked clothing insulates nothing). Stay **cold and wet** for a few minutes — caught in freezing rain, or after falling through the ice — and a **chill sets in**: a lingering [sickness](./injuries-and-conditions.md#sickness-foodborne-illness) that stays with you even after you've warmed up. **Dry off or get to a fire** before it takes hold; an herbal remedy will treat it once it has.

### Heat → heatstroke

Overheat and you take **burning damage** as if you were on fire. Standing in lava or a desert at noon in heavy clothing will push you there fast.

## What warms and cools you

- **Wetness multiplies cold — and ruins your clothing's warmth.** Soaked hide and wool hold **almost no
  heat** (they keep only about **a quarter** of their insulation until they dry), so being **wet in the
  cold is dangerous even fully dressed**. This is why wet-cold, not dry-cold, is the real killer — and why
  drying off by a fire is worth more than another layer.
- **A towel dries you at once.** You normally dry over ~30 seconds (faster by a fire), but a **towel**
  (woven from **wool**) lets you **right-click to rub yourself dry instantly** — the fast way to kill the
  wet-cold after you climb out of a river or duck out of a downpour. Using it soaks the towel, so it goes
  **damp** and needs about a minute to dry before it'll work again; and while you're still *in* the rain or
  water it just re-wets, so it's what you reach for the moment you get **out** of the wet.
- **A fire dries you** about five times faster than air alone, and warms you at the same time.
- **Heat sources warm you:** stand near a **lit campfire**, open **fire**, **torches** <img class="mc-icon" src="/alone/item/torch.png" alt="Torch"/>, or **lava** and the square climbs back toward green. Lava is strong enough to overheat you.
- **Shelter insulates — and walls matter.** Being under cover (no open sky above you) shields you from rain and the cold night sky. A **bare roof** (an open lean-to) pulls the temperature about **40% toward comfortable**, but the more **walled in** you are the better it holds heat: a **fully enclosed shelter** (a solid wall within about 4 blocks on all four sides) reaches roughly **78%**. So building real walls, not just a roof, is what makes a cabin beat winter.
- **Thatch is the day-one roof — and it's real work.** Any solid block overhead counts as a roof, but you rarely have planks or spare dirt on night one. **Thatch** is the primitive answer: lay it over a dug-out niche or a low wall of logs and the space beneath is roofed and, with the sky blocked, **rain-free**, so you stay dry (the thing that keeps wet-cold from killing you). But it isn't a free craft — it's the labour that keeps a first shelter *small*: you thatch it from **leaf litter** (torn from the canopy by hand — slow, tugging work) bundled onto **stick** battens and lashed with a little **cordage**, and the **weaving is a timed job** at the bench (about a quarter-hour of compressed work per couple of panels). The other catch: thatch is **bone-dry tinder** — it catches readily and **fire runs across a thatch roof**, so keep it well clear of your campfire.
- **The tarp is the roof you can put over a fire.** A **tarp** — a sewn, oiled hide sheet (**leather + cordage**, so it's real gear you make once you're hunting and tanning, or bring from the start) — roofs and dries you just like thatch, but it's the **premium, packable** version: it **doesn't burn**, so unlike thatch you can pitch it straight over a campfire and have flame *and* a dry roof together, and you **pick it back up** and carry it on. Hard to make, but the shelter that travels with you.
- **Blizzards are deadly in the open.** A **winter storm** (a thunderstorm while it's winter) is a
  blizzard — driving snow and wind that **freezes the exposed fast**. Caught out under open sky it piles a
  hard extra chill on top of the season's cold and **slows you to a trudge** through the whiteout, with a
  warning to get to cover. **Any roof breaks the worst of it** (you're out of the driving snow), and a
  fire under that roof does the rest — so a blizzard is a race to shelter, exactly as it should be.
- **Caves are cold, not a refuge.** Underground air is stagnant and damp, and the deeper you go the colder it locks — near the surface (the top ~15m) a cave just moderates the weather above, but below that it settles into a constant, clammy chill (and colder still toward bedrock, a genuine hypothermia zone). It's survivable while you're **dry and moving and dressed for it**, but being **wet or standing still** turns a deep cave dangerous fast. So a long mining trip is a cold-exposure problem: **wear your hides, keep dry, and bring fire** (a torch or campfire warms the space) — exactly the way real cavers dread the cold more than the dark.
- **Hot meals warm you.** Eating a cooked meal or stew bumps your body temperature toward comfort (it won't overheat you), then drifts back over about **40 seconds**.
- **A cool drink eases the heat.** Drinking water while you're **overheated** lowers your temperature back toward comfort (it can't chill you below it). So water is doubly precious in the heat — it quenches thirst *and* cools you — which is why a desert run lives or dies on it. (Salt water still only dehydrates.)

## Clothing

What you wear shifts the temperature loop:

- **Leather and hide** (armor pieces and turtle shell) **insulate well**. In the cold they blunt the chill back toward comfort. In the **heat** they trap warmth and make you **overheat faster**.
- **Metal plate** barely insulates, and under an **open sky in the heat** it **bakes you** — the sun turns your armor into an oven. **Shade cuts the bake:** a roof stops it entirely, and **tree cover dapples it** — standing under **~3 leaf layers** roughly **halves** the sun-heat, falling off with a denser canopy.
- **Wet clothing insulates nothing** — soaked layers do nothing until they dry.

## How temperature feeds your other meters

Temperature isn't isolated — it drives the rest of your body:

- **Heat → sweat → thirst.** The hotter your *body* runs — from desert sun, standing by a fire, heavy clothing, or hard exertion — the faster your **thirst drains**.
- **Cold → extra hunger + slow recovery.** A cold body **burns extra food** to keep its heat (the colder, the more), and once you're badly chilled your **stamina recovers at half rate**.
