Your body runs at a temperature you have to manage — too cold and you freeze, too hot and you cook — and it quietly drives your thirst, hunger, and stamina alongside its own dangers.

## Reading the temperature square

To the right of the corner meters is a **temperature square** with a small **trend arrow**:

- **Blue** — you're cold; **darker blue** means dangerously cold.
- **Green** — comfortable, right where you want to be.
- **Red** — you're hot; **darker red** means dangerously hot.
- The **arrow** shows the direction you're heading: warming up or cooling down. Watch it — it tells you whether what you're doing is working before the color even changes.

## What drives your temperature

Your body temperature is pushed around by your surroundings:

- **Biome** — snowy taigas and tundras chill you; deserts and badlands bake you.
- **Weather** — rain and storms chill you fast.
- **Season** — winter shifts everything colder, summer warmer.
- **Time of day** — the open night sky is cold.
- **Wetness** — being wet or submerged makes cold far worse (see below).

## The danger bands

### Cold → hypothermia

Linger in the cold and you first **slow down**, then start taking **freezing damage**. Cold also has knock-on costs before it's outright deadly: below a hard chill your **stamina recovers at half rate** (you're stiff and shivering) and your body **burns extra food** to hold its heat.

### Heat → heatstroke

Overheat and you take **burning damage** as if you were on fire. Standing in lava or a desert at noon in heavy clothing will push you there fast.

## Staying warm

- **Wetness multiplies cold.** Falling into a winter river is an immediate emergency — get out and dry off. **Wet clothing insulates nothing.**
- **A fire dries you** about five times faster than air alone, and warms you at the same time.
- **Heat sources warm you:** stand near a **lit campfire**, open **fire**, **torches**, or **lava** and the square climbs back toward green. Lava is strong enough to overheat you, so don't hug it.
- **Shelter insulates.** Being under cover (no open sky above you) shields you from rain and the cold night sky and pulls the surrounding temperature about **40% toward comfortable**. Deep underground stays a stable, mild cave temperature. A roof plus a fire is how you beat winter.
- **Hot meals warm you.** Eating a cooked meal or stew bumps your body temperature toward comfort (it won't overheat you), then drifts back over about **40 seconds**. A hot dinner is a real tool against the cold.

## Clothing: dress for the season

What you wear shifts the temperature loop, and the trade-offs are real:

- **Leather and hide** (armor pieces and turtle shell) **insulate well**. In the cold they blunt the chill back toward comfort. But those layers can't be shed in a hurry — in the **heat** they trap warmth and make you **overheat faster**.
- **Metal plate** barely insulates, and under an **open sky in the heat** it **bakes you** — the sun turns your armor into an oven.
- **Wet clothing insulates nothing** — soaked layers do you no good until they dry.

The lesson: warm layers for winter, shed them or wear light for the desert, and keep everything dry.

## How temperature feeds your other meters

Temperature isn't isolated — it drives the rest of your body:

- **Heat → sweat → thirst.** The hotter your *body* runs — from desert sun, standing by a fire, heavy clothing, or hard exertion — the faster your **thirst drains**. Working hot costs water.
- **Cold → extra hunger + slow recovery.** A cold body **burns extra food** to keep its heat (the colder, the more), and once you're badly chilled your **stamina recovers at half rate**.

See [Survival Meters & HUD](./survival-meters.md) for the meters this feeds into.

## Practical survival

**Surviving winter cold:**

- Build a **roof over your camp** and keep a **fire** going — shelter plus a heat source is the core of it.
- **Stay dry.** Avoid falling in water; if you get wet, dry by the fire before anything else.
- Wear **leather or hide** layers.
- Keep **cooked meals** on hand for a warming boost, and stockpile extra food — cold makes you eat more.

**Surviving desert heat:**

- Travel **light** — leave heavy leather and metal off, or seek shade.
- Carry **plenty of water**; heat drains thirst fast, and your reserves go quickly.
- Avoid **standing in the open sun in metal armor** — it will cook you.
- Get **under cover** during the hottest stretches.
