Fire is your first and most important technology — it warms you, cooks your food, and boils your water clean. It is never free, and keeping one alive is easier than starting a new one.

## Making fire from friction

There is no day-one flint-and-steel. Your first fire comes from friction:

- **Crouch and hold right-click** with a stick on the ground to drill for an ember.
- Drilling alone only makes **smoke** — it won't catch.
- To actually light a fire you need **tinder** on hand: a bundle of **plant fiber**, or dry **leaf litter**. The tinder is **consumed** when the fire catches.
- **Wet materials and rain make it fail.** Getting soaked at dusk with no fire is the classic survival emergency — gather and keep your tinder dry.

So the routine is: gather tinder first, find dry ground, then drill.

## The campfire

The campfire is your hearth. **Craft it from sticks over logs** — no coal needed.

Once lit, it runs on fuel and needs feeding:

- **Right-click it with sticks, logs, or planks** to add fuel and make it burn longer.
- It **burns down over time and goes out** when the fuel runs dry.
- It gives off **warmth that dwindles as the fuel runs low** — a dying fire warms you less, so keep it fed on a cold night.
- It **cooks food** and **boils water** (set a fire-safe vessel over it — see [Water](./water-and-drinking.md)).

## Carrying an ember

Rather than drill a fresh fire at every new camp, you can carry fire with you — a real survival technique.

- **Sneak and right-click a lit campfire empty-handed** to scoop out a glowing **ember**. This costs the fire a little fuel.
- The ember **cools as you carry it**. Its durability-style bar shows the remaining glow — it lasts about **3.3 minutes** before it dies to cold charcoal.
- **Right-click the ground** with a live ember to start a **fresh, already-lit campfire** — no drilling, no tinder needed.
- Let the ember run out before you use it and it dies. Move promptly.

Carrying an ember between camps saves you the whole friction-fire ordeal, so a banked fire is worth more than a cold pile of wood.
